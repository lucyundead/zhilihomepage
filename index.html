Theoretical and Computational Astrophysicist

I am a TDLI Prize Postdoc fellow at Tsung-Dao Li Institute, Shanghai Jiao Tong University. My current research interests focus on gas dynamics in our Milky Way and nearby isolated galaxies using numerical simulations. This would help us better understand many problems as the origin of various features in galaxies, the formation of supermassive black holes, and the nature of dark matter. Ongoing Works


Gas Flows in Double-Barred Galaxies

Galaxies with an inner (smaller) bar that is nested inside an outer (larger) bar are called double-barred galaxies. These type of galaxies have unique dynamical properties and may help regulating the evolution of central black holes. With the help of high-resolution numerical simulation, I am investigating the gas flow patterns in a self-consistent double-barred galaxy model and see whether this system could drive gas from a few kpc down to central parsecs.


Dynamics of the Nuclear Stellar Disk and the Central Molecular Zone

Thick dust that shrouds the galactic center limits our knowledge on the Nuclear Stellar Disk (NSD) and Central Molecular Zone (CMZ), which lie at the very center of the Milky Way with a diameter of ~100-200 pc. Observations suggest that CMZ has a twisted ∞ shape viewed from the Sun, and its current star formation rate is quite low considering its high gas density. However, other studies have found that NSD is made of stars born in the dense CMZ gas, leaving the question of what regulates the star formation in extreme environments like CMZ. I am investigating what is the mechanism o form a self-consistent CMZ+NSD in a typical Milky Way mass galaxy, and how can we constrain the current star formation theories by studying the properties of CMZ and NSD.


Peculiar Motions of Masers in the MW

Recent BeSSeL2 result measured the 3D velocity and position of nearly 200 masers in the Milky Way with high-precision VLBI data. Some masers are found to have unexpected large non-circular peculiar motions (~50 km/s) around Persus arm and the Galactic bar end. This may related to the large-scale dynamics of the non-axis symmetric components of the Galaxy and is still not well-understood yet. With several galactic models, I am investigating the mechanisim to produce such masers with large peculiar motions and how could we learn more about our Galaxy from these models.

Research Highlights


Constraining Fuzzy Dark Matter Model

Using the kinematics and shape of the gas in the central part of the Milky Way, we tried to constrain the particle mass of Fuzzy Dark Matter (FDM), a theory that describes the dark matter as very light bosons that have de broglie wavelength on galactic scales. Our results favours a boson mass of 2-7×10-22eV, and this range could be further improved if we have a better detemination of the mass-to-light ratio of the stars at the Galactic Center. This work was highlighted by AAS NOVA.


Acoustic Instability of Gas Disks

The acoustic instability was proposed to be the mechanism to drive turbulence in the Milky Way’s Central Molecular Zone. We found this instability is a spurious result, at least for the case of non-self-gravitating gas with isothermal EoS. Perturbations that are predicted to be acoustically unstable wind up and eventually propagate off to infinity, so that the system is actually stable. On the other hand, a "geometric growth" of the perturbation wave may happen if the group velocity of the wave propogrates inwards from outer region.


Stability of M33

We constructed N-body+hydro models for the Local Group galaxy M33. We found that the current state of M33 is probably unstable, which leads to the formation of a strong bar in our models within 1 Gyr. However, the morphology of gas disk in our models well-matched the observed one even after the bar formation, leaving interesting questions on how the two disks interact with each other. A movie of gas surface density in our model could be found here. The survival of the current state of this exceptionally well-studied galaxy is not yet understood. We also suspect that many other unbarred galaxies present a similar puzzle.


Rapid Formation of Suppermassive Black Holes

We argue in this study that bars drive gas right into the nucleus in the early stages of disk galaxy formation, where a nuclear star cluster and perhaps a massive black hole could be created. The process is self-limiting, however, because inflow stalls at a nuclear ring once the mass of gas and stars in the nucleus exceeds ∼1% of the disk mass, which shuts off rapid growth of the black hole. The less massive SMBHs in disk galaxies that lack a significant classical bulge (e.g. the Milky Way) may originate from such a scenario. This work was also on some newspapers and websites.


Gas Dynamics in the Milky Way

How could we know the morphology of our own galaxy as we see it from edge-on? To answer this question, we have to combine the observed gas density and kinematics and build dynamical models. We present gas flow models for the Milky Way based on realistic galactic potentials constrained by the density of red clump giants in the Galactic bulge. We can reproduce many features in the observed longitude-velocity diagram with a bar pattern speed of 33 km/s/kpc and a spiral pattern speed of 23 km/s/kpc. The shape and kinematics of the nuclear ring, Bania’s Clump 2, the Connecting arm, the Near and Far 3-kpc arms, the Molecular Ring, and the spiral arm tangent points in our simulations are comparable to those in the observations. Our result favours that a low bar pattern speed for the Milky Way compared to previous gas models, but this value is more consistent with other independent studies.


Nuclear Rings in Barred Galaxies

We studied the formation of nuclear rings in barred galaxies by hydrodynamical simulations. The nuclear rings formed in our simulations can be divided into two groups: one group is nearly round and the other is highly elongated. We find that roundish rings may not form when the bar pattern speed is too high or the bulge central density is too low. We also study the periodic orbits in our galaxy models, and find that the concept of inner Lindblad resonance (ILR) may be generalized by the extent of x2 orbits. We found nuclear rings form exactly at the radius where the residual angular momentum of infalling gas balances the centrifugal force. Our result may have important implications for using nuclear rings to measure the parameters of real barred galaxies with 2D IFU gas kinematics.

SPH and Grid-based Codes on Galactic Scales

The movie shows a comparison for simulating gas flows in a rotating barred potential, by using a SPH code Phantom and a grid-based code athena. The potential is adopted to be the same as the canonical model in Li et al. (2015) . The gas behaviors in both codes are similar but the differences are also clear, especially at the centeral part.


Modelling M33

This video shows the evolution of the projected gas surface density in our M33 model. The model includes multiphase gas, star formation & stellar feedback, and many other physics modules, which leads to the formation of various interesting gas structures. The morphology of our gas disk is quite similar to the observed one at some snapshots.
