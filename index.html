About

How gas shapes galaxies

I study gas dynamics in the Milky Way and nearby isolated galaxies using numerical simulations.

My work explores the origin of galactic structures, the formation of supermassive black holes, and the nature of dark matter. I combine dynamical models with observations to understand how galaxies evolve from their largest structures down to their central regions.

Current questions

Research

Numerical experiments connect the observable structure of galaxies to the physical processes operating within them.

01

Gas Flows in Double-Barred Galaxies

Galaxies with an inner bar nested inside an outer bar have unique dynamical properties and may help regulate the evolution of central black holes. With high-resolution numerical simulations, I investigate gas-flow patterns in a self-consistent double-barred galaxy and whether they can drive gas from kiloparsec scales down to the central parsecs.

02

Nuclear Stellar Disk and Central Molecular Zone

Dust obscuration limits our knowledge of the Nuclear Stellar Disk and Central Molecular Zone at the center of the Milky Way. I investigate how a self-consistent CMZ and NSD form in a Milky Way-mass galaxy, what regulates star formation in this extreme environment, and how their properties can constrain current star-formation theories.

03

Peculiar Motions of Masers in the Milky Way

High-precision VLBI measurements reveal masers with unexpectedly large non-circular motions near the Perseus arm and the end of the Galactic bar. With several Galactic models, I investigate the mechanisms that produce these motions and what they can tell us about the Milky Way's non-axisymmetric structure.

Selected results

Research highlights

Constraining Fuzzy Dark Matter

Gas kinematics and morphology in the Milky Way center favor a boson mass of 2–7 × 10−22 eV. Better stellar mass-to-light measurements could tighten this range. The work was highlighted by AAS Nova.

Acoustic Instability of Gas Disks

We found that the proposed acoustic instability is spurious for non-self-gravitating isothermal gas. Predicted unstable perturbations wind up and propagate away, while geometric growth can occur for waves whose group velocity moves inward.

Stability of M33

Our N-body and hydrodynamic models suggest that M33's present state is unstable and forms a strong bar within 1 Gyr. The simulated gas disk can nevertheless resemble observations, leaving a puzzle about how M33 has retained its current unbarred appearance.

Rapid Formation of Supermassive Black Holes

Bars can drive gas into galactic nuclei early in disk formation, building a nuclear star cluster and perhaps a massive black hole. The process becomes self-limiting when central mass growth stalls inflow at a nuclear ring.

Gas Dynamics in the Milky Way

Realistic gas-flow models reproduce key structures in the observed longitude-velocity diagram with a low Galactic bar pattern speed. The result aligns the gas morphology with independent dynamical measurements.

Nuclear Rings in Barred Galaxies

Hydrodynamical simulations show how bar pattern speed, central density, periodic orbits, and residual angular momentum determine the shape and radius of nuclear rings. These rings can help constrain real barred galaxies.

Simulation Movies

Dynamic views of gas flows and galactic evolution.

SPH and Grid-Based Codes on Galactic Scales

A comparison of gas flows in a rotating barred potential using the SPH code Phantom and the grid-based code Athena. Both reproduce similar large-scale behavior, with visible differences in the central region.

Related paper

Modelling M33

The evolution of projected gas surface density in a multiphase M33 model with star formation, stellar feedback, and other physical processes. At some snapshots, the simulated gas morphology closely resembles observations.
